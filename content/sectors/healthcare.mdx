## Overview

Nigeria's healthcare sector is characterised by enormous unmet demand, low government spending relative to WHO benchmarks, and a heavy reliance on imported pharmaceuticals and medical equipment. On the NGX, the sector is represented primarily by domestic pharmaceutical manufacturers, with Fidson Healthcare (FIDSON) and May & Baker Nigeria (MAYBAKER) being the most actively traded names.

Nigeria spends roughly 3-4% of GDP on healthcare, well below the 5% minimum recommended by the WHO. Out-of-pocket expenditure accounts for the majority of health spending, as national health insurance coverage remains limited. This underfunding creates both a challenge -- limited public procurement budgets -- and an opportunity, as any meaningful increase in healthcare spending flows directly to drug manufacturers, distributors, and service providers.

The pharmaceutical manufacturing subsector has been buoyed by government policies encouraging local drug production. The National Agency for Food and Drug Administration and Control (NAFDAC) regulates product registration and quality standards, while the federal government has introduced incentives such as import duty waivers on raw materials and preferential procurement of locally manufactured drugs. These policies aim to reduce Nigeria's estimated $1.5 billion annual pharmaceutical import bill and build domestic manufacturing capability.

## Key Drivers

- **Population and disease burden**: Nigeria's large and growing population, combined with a high burden of infectious and chronic diseases, sustains demand for pharmaceuticals.
- **Local manufacturing incentives**: Government policies favouring locally produced drugs over imports provide a competitive moat for domestic manufacturers.
- **Healthcare reform**: Expansion of the National Health Insurance Authority (NHIA) and state-level health insurance schemes could unlock significant new demand.
- **WHO prequalification**: Companies achieving WHO prequalification can access international donor-funded procurement, expanding their addressable market across Africa.
- **R&D and product pipeline**: Companies investing in new formulations, therapeutic areas, and higher-margin specialty drugs can drive revenue growth beyond commodity generics.

## Key Risks

- **FX exposure on raw materials**: Most active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) are imported, making production costs sensitive to naira depreciation.
- **Regulatory delays**: NAFDAC product registration timelines and compliance requirements can slow new product launches.
- **Competition from imports**: Despite protectionist policies, smuggled and informally imported drugs continue to compete with locally manufactured products.
- **Limited sector depth**: With only two actively traded stocks, sector-level diversification is minimal.

## Notable Events

- Fidson Healthcare commissioned its expanded manufacturing facility, increasing capacity for infusions, tablets, and capsules, and moving closer to WHO prequalification standards.
- May & Baker Nigeria diversified its revenue streams, expanding into food and beverage segments alongside its core pharmaceutical business.
- The federal government reiterated its commitment to the "pharma is the new oil" initiative, aiming to make Nigeria a regional pharmaceutical manufacturing hub.

## Outlook

The healthcare sector offers asymmetric upside for investors willing to take a long-term view. Any meaningful increase in government health spending, expansion of insurance coverage, or successful export push into West African markets could catalyse significant earnings growth for domestic manufacturers. Near-term performance hinges on FX costs, raw material availability, and the pace of policy implementation. The sector remains a niche but strategically important corner of the NGX.
