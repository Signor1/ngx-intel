## Overview

The banking sector is the backbone of the Nigerian Stock Exchange, accounting for the largest share of market capitalisation and daily trading volume. Nigerian banks serve as the primary channel for financial intermediation in Africa's largest economy, connecting savers to borrowers and facilitating domestic and cross-border commerce. The sector is dominated by Tier-1 names such as Zenith Bank, Guaranty Trust Holding Company (GTCO), United Bank for Africa (UBA), Access Holdings (ACCESSCORP), and FBN Holdings (FBNH), each commanding balance sheets in the trillions of naira.

The Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) serves as the primary regulator, setting capital requirements, monetary policy rates, and prudential guidelines that shape bank profitability and risk appetite. In recent years the sector has undergone significant structural change driven by the CBN's recapitalisation directive, which requires commercial banks with international authorisation to raise their minimum capital base to N500 billion by March 2026. This has triggered a wave of capital raises, rights issues, and strategic mergers that have reshaped the competitive landscape.

Digital banking continues to redefine service delivery. Mobile money, agency banking, and fintech partnerships have expanded financial inclusion beyond traditional branch networks. Banks are investing heavily in technology infrastructure to compete with nimble fintech challengers while leveraging their regulatory advantages and deposit franchises.

## Key Drivers

- **Recapitalisation exercise**: The ongoing capital raise is strengthening balance sheets and positioning banks for larger ticket lending, especially to the oil and gas and infrastructure sectors.
- **Interest rate environment**: The CBN's monetary policy rate directly influences net interest margins. Higher rates generally boost bank earnings on risk-free government securities.
- **Digital transformation**: Investment in USSD banking, mobile apps, and API-driven partnerships is lowering cost-to-serve and opening new revenue streams such as payment processing and transaction fees.
- **Foreign exchange policy**: Naira volatility and FX revaluation gains (or losses) can materially swing quarterly earnings for banks with large foreign-currency-denominated assets.
- **Financial inclusion push**: Government and CBN initiatives to bank the unbanked expand the addressable market for deposits, micro-lending, and insurance cross-selling.

## Key Risks

- **Asset quality deterioration**: Economic slowdowns or sector-specific shocks (oil price crashes, power sector debt) can spike non-performing loan ratios.
- **Regulatory tightening**: Sudden changes in CRR (Cash Reserve Ratio), LDR (Loan-to-Deposit Ratio), or capital adequacy requirements can compress margins or force rapid capital deployment.
- **Cybersecurity and fraud**: As digital channels expand, so does exposure to electronic fraud, system outages, and data breaches.
- **Naira devaluation**: While revaluation gains can boost earnings, sustained currency weakness erodes the real value of naira-denominated profits for foreign investors.

## Notable Events

- The CBN's March 2024 recapitalisation directive set a two-year deadline that has dominated sector headlines and capital market activity through 2025 and into 2026.
- Several Tier-2 banks have explored mergers to meet the new capital thresholds, potentially reducing the total number of licensed banks.
- GTCO and Zenith Bank completed landmark public offers in 2025, drawing strong institutional and retail participation.

## Outlook

The banking sector enters 2026 on firm footing. Recapitalisation is strengthening buffers, digital channels are maturing, and a gradually stabilising macroeconomic environment supports credit growth. Investors should watch for post-recapitalisation earnings dilution, integration risks from mergers, and the trajectory of the monetary policy rate. Banks that successfully blend scale, technology, and prudent risk management are best positioned to deliver sustained shareholder returns.
