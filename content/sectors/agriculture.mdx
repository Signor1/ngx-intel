## Overview

Agriculture is the largest employer in Nigeria, engaging roughly 35% of the labour force, yet the sector's representation on the NGX is surprisingly narrow. The two most prominent listed agribusiness companies are Presco (PRESCO) and Okomu Oil Palm Company (OKOMUOIL), both of which are vertically integrated palm oil producers with large plantation estates in Edo and Delta states. Together they dominate the listed agriculture space, benefiting from Nigeria's structural deficit in edible oils and the government's push to reduce food imports.

Palm oil is Nigeria's most important agricultural commodity in the capital markets context. The country was once the world's largest producer but has since fallen behind Malaysia and Indonesia due to decades of underinvestment. Local demand far exceeds domestic supply, creating a persistent import gap that supports strong pricing for local producers. Both Presco and Okomu sell primarily into the domestic market, insulating them from global price volatility to some extent while benefiting from naira weakness that makes imports more expensive.

Beyond palm oil, Nigeria's broader agricultural sector encompasses cocoa, cashew nuts, sesame, rubber, and staple crops like rice, maize, and cassava. However, most of these subsectors are dominated by unlisted smallholders and cooperatives rather than publicly traded corporations.

## Key Drivers

- **Domestic palm oil demand**: Nigeria's large population and food processing industry create consistent demand for crude and refined palm oil, with local supply falling short of consumption.
- **Import substitution policies**: Government restrictions on palm oil imports and the CBN's exclusion of certain agricultural imports from official FX windows support domestic producers.
- **Commodity prices**: Local palm oil prices are influenced by global benchmarks, domestic supply-demand dynamics, and the naira exchange rate.
- **Plantation expansion**: Both Presco and Okomu are investing in new plantings and replanting of ageing palms to increase future yield per hectare.
- **Food security agenda**: Federal government programmes aimed at boosting agricultural productivity create a supportive policy environment.

## Key Risks

- **Land and community disputes**: Plantation expansion can trigger conflicts with host communities over land rights, compensation, and environmental concerns.
- **Climate and weather**: Rainfall variability, flooding, and pest infestations can significantly affect crop yields in any given year.
- **Seasonal earnings volatility**: Harvesting cycles create quarterly revenue lumpiness that can mislead short-term investors.
- **Limited sector depth**: With only two major listed companies, the sector offers little diversification and is vulnerable to company-specific events.

## Notable Events

- Presco expanded its plantation acreage and invested in milling capacity upgrades to process a larger share of its fresh fruit bunches in-house.
- Okomu Oil Palm delivered strong revenue growth driven by elevated domestic palm oil prices and improved yield from maturing plantations.
- The federal government renewed its commitment to agricultural import substitution, maintaining protective trade policies that benefit local producers.

## Outlook

The agriculture sector offers a compelling long-term story rooted in Nigeria's food security needs and import substitution drive. Presco and Okomu are well-positioned as low-cost domestic producers in a supply-deficit market. Investors should expect some earnings cyclicality tied to harvest quality and commodity prices, but the structural demand backdrop remains supportive. Any expansion in listed agribusiness names through new listings would be a welcome development for sector depth.
