## Overview

The Nigerian insurance sector remains one of the most underpenetrated in the world, with gross premium as a percentage of GDP hovering below 1%, compared to the global average of roughly 7% and South Africa's 13%. This low penetration represents both the sector's core challenge and its most compelling investment thesis. Listed insurers on the NGX include AIICO Insurance, AXA Mansard (MANSARD), NEM Insurance, and Custodian Investment.

The National Insurance Commission (NAICOM) is the industry regulator, responsible for licensing, capital requirements, and consumer protection. NAICOM has been pushing for higher minimum capital requirements to strengthen the sector and encourage consolidation, mirroring the CBN's approach in banking. A recapitalisation exercise is expected to reduce the number of underwriting firms while creating better-capitalised entities capable of retaining larger risks domestically.

Insurance products in Nigeria span life, general (motor, fire, marine), and specialised lines such as oil and gas, agriculture, and micro-insurance. Distribution has historically been agent-driven, but insurtech platforms and bancassurance partnerships are beginning to modernise the channel mix. Awareness campaigns and compulsory insurance enforcement are gradually expanding the market.

## Key Drivers

- **Low penetration upside**: With penetration below 1%, even modest improvements in uptake translate to significant premium growth.
- **Recapitalisation**: Higher capital requirements are strengthening balance sheets and enabling insurers to underwrite larger, more complex risks locally rather than ceding them to reinsurers.
- **Regulatory enforcement**: Stricter enforcement of compulsory insurance lines (motor third-party, building insurance, group life) expands the premium pool.
- **Bancassurance and insurtech**: Partnerships with banks and digital platforms are reducing distribution costs and reaching previously unserved demographics.
- **Investment income**: Insurers generate significant returns from their investment portfolios, particularly fixed-income securities, which benefit from high interest rate environments.

## Key Risks

- **Claims fraud and moral hazard**: Weak claims management systems and fraud inflate loss ratios and erode profitability.
- **Low public trust**: Many Nigerians view insurance with scepticism due to historical claims settlement delays, limiting voluntary uptake.
- **Underpricing competition**: Intense competition among numerous small insurers leads to premium rate cutting that undermines sector profitability.
- **Regulatory capital demands**: Smaller firms may struggle to meet new capital thresholds, forcing distressed mergers or licence revocations.

## Notable Events

- NAICOM announced plans to raise minimum capital requirements for insurers, setting a timeline that has prompted early-stage merger discussions across the sector.
- AXA Mansard continued to leverage its bancassurance partnership with Access Bank to grow retail premium volumes.
- NEM Insurance delivered consistent underwriting profitability, distinguishing itself as one of the sector's most disciplined operators.

## Outlook

The insurance sector is poised for structural transformation. Recapitalisation will create fewer, stronger players. Rising awareness, regulatory enforcement, and digital distribution are expected to steadily lift penetration rates. Near-term earnings may be volatile as companies absorb recapitalisation costs and navigate competitive pressures, but the long-term growth trajectory is among the most attractive on the NGX for patient investors.
