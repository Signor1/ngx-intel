## A Year Defined by Capital Raises

If there is a single theme that defined the Nigerian Exchange (NGX) in 2025, it was banking recapitalisation. The Central Bank of Nigeria's March 2024 directive requiring commercial banks with international authorisation to raise their minimum capital base to N500 billion set off a chain of corporate actions that dominated headlines, trading volumes, and investor attention throughout the year. Rights issues, public offers, and private placements from the country's biggest banks became the primary engine of market activity.

The NGX All-Share Index (ASI) delivered another positive year, building on the strong momentum of 2024. While the double-digit gains of the previous year were hard to replicate, the market posted respectable returns driven by banking sector capital raises, improved corporate earnings, and a gradual stabilisation of the macroeconomic environment following the reforms of 2023-2024.

## Banking Sector: Centre Stage

The recapitalisation exercise was the defining event of the year. GTCO, Zenith Bank, Access Holdings, and FBN Holdings all launched capital raises of varying structures, from rights issues to public offers. The sheer scale of capital mobilisation was unprecedented, with the banking sector collectively raising trillions of naira from domestic and international investors.

These exercises had several effects on the broader market. First, they drew significant liquidity into the equities market as both institutional and retail investors participated in discounted offers. Second, they created short-term selling pressure as some shareholders sold existing holdings to fund their rights take-up. Third, they expanded the free float of major banking stocks, improving long-term liquidity and potential index weight.

Beyond the capital raises themselves, banking sector earnings were robust. Net interest margins benefited from elevated monetary policy rates, while foreign exchange revaluation gains -- a recurring theme since the naira float -- continued to boost bottom lines. However, there were signs that FX gains were normalising, prompting investors to focus more closely on core operating performance.

## The Macro Backdrop

The macroeconomic environment in 2025 was one of cautious optimism. Inflation, while still elevated, showed signs of deceleration from the peaks seen in 2024. The naira, after a volatile period following the unification of exchange rate windows, found a degree of stability, reducing the uncertainty that had weighed on foreign portfolio investment.

The CBN maintained a tight monetary policy stance, keeping the Monetary Policy Rate (MPR) elevated to anchor inflation expectations. This high-rate environment was a double-edged sword: it supported bank earnings through higher yields on government securities and wider lending spreads, but it also raised borrowing costs for corporates and consumers, weighing on non-financial sector earnings and economic growth.

Oil prices remained supportive, hovering in the $75-85 per barrel range for much of the year, providing a floor for government revenue and foreign exchange inflows. The Dangote Refinery continued its phased ramp-up, processing increasing volumes of crude and beginning to reduce Nigeria's dependence on imported refined petroleum products.

## Sector Performances

Beyond banking, several sectors delivered notable performance. The oil and gas sector benefited from Seplat Energy's acquisition of ExxonMobil's shallow-water assets and the listing of Aradel Holdings, which added depth to the listed E&P universe. Industrial goods remained a heavyweight, with Dangote Cement and BUA Cement maintaining their dominance despite rising energy costs.

The consumer goods sector had a mixed year. Companies like BUA Foods continued to grow, but legacy FMCG names struggled with input cost inflation and consumer downtrading. The ICT sector, anchored by MTN Nigeria, saw data revenue growth continue to outpace voice, while the launch of MoMo PSB marked an important strategic pivot toward financial services.

The insurance and healthcare sectors remained niche but showed signs of life, with recapitalisation discussions in insurance and capacity expansion in pharmaceuticals generating investor interest.

## Foreign Investor Activity

Foreign portfolio investment (FPI) showed modest improvement in 2025, though it remained well below pre-2020 levels. The stabilisation of the exchange rate and improved FX liquidity at the official window were positive signals, but elevated country risk premiums and competing opportunities in other frontier and emerging markets limited the pace of foreign capital return. Domestic investors, particularly pension funds and asset managers, continued to drive the majority of market turnover.

## Lessons and Takeaways

The 2025 market reinforced several enduring lessons for NGX investors. First, regulatory catalysts -- in this case, CBN recapitalisation directives -- can create powerful investment themes that override broader macroeconomic conditions. Second, earnings quality matters: as FX revaluation gains normalise, investors will increasingly reward banks and corporates that demonstrate sustainable operating performance. Third, market breadth is improving -- new listings like Aradel and the listing of Transcorp Power expanded the investable universe, a trend that bodes well for long-term market development.

## Looking Ahead to 2026

As the recapitalisation deadline approaches in early 2026, investors will be watching for the final wave of capital raises, potential mergers among smaller banks, and the post-recapitalisation earnings outlook. Will bigger balance sheets translate into stronger lending growth and better returns, or will dilution weigh on per-share metrics? The answer will shape banking sector valuations for years to come.

Beyond banking, the trajectory of inflation, the sustainability of naira stability, and the pace of Dangote Refinery's output ramp-up will be the key macro variables to watch. The NGX enters 2026 with improving fundamentals, a broader listed universe, and growing retail investor participation -- all positive ingredients for continued market development.
