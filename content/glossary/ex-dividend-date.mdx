The **ex-dividend date** is the cutoff date that determines whether a shareholder is entitled to receive a company's upcoming dividend payment. If you buy a stock on or after the ex-dividend date, you will **not** receive the next dividend. You must own the shares **before** this date to qualify.

## How It Works on the NGX

When a Nigerian company declares a dividend, several important dates are announced:

1. **Declaration date**: The date the company's board announces the dividend.
2. **Qualification date (Record date)**: The date by which you must be registered as a shareholder in the company's books to receive the dividend.
3. **Ex-dividend date**: Typically set one or two business days before the qualification date. This is the key date for traders.
4. **Payment date**: The date the dividend is actually paid into your bank account.

## A Nigerian Example

Suppose **GTCO** declares a dividend of ₦3.00 per share with an ex-dividend date of March 15. If you buy GTCO shares on March 14 (one day before), you qualify for the dividend. If you buy on March 15 or later, you do not.

## What Happens to the Share Price?

On the ex-dividend date, the stock price typically drops by approximately the amount of the dividend. This is called the **ex-dividend adjustment**. If GTCO was trading at ₦43 and the dividend is ₦3.00, the share price may open around ₦40 on the ex-dividend date. This is normal and reflects the fact that new buyers no longer have a claim to that particular dividend.

## Why It Matters

Understanding the ex-dividend date is crucial for two strategies. **Income investors** ensure they buy before this date to collect dividends. **Traders** sometimes buy before and sell after, or wait to buy at the adjusted lower price after the ex-date.

## Key Takeaway

Always check the ex-dividend date before buying a stock if receiving the dividend matters to you. This information is typically published by the NGX and financial news platforms.
