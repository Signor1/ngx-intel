Think of it this way: two people each own a house worth ₦30 million. Person A paid ₦25 million from savings and took a ₦5 million mortgage. Person B paid ₦5 million from savings and took a ₦25 million mortgage. Both own the same house, but Person B is far more leveraged — they owe five times what they put in. If house prices drop, Person B is in serious trouble. The **debt-to-equity ratio** measures exactly this kind of leverage for companies.

The **Debt-to-Equity (D/E) ratio** compares how much a company has borrowed (debt) to how much the shareholders have invested (equity). It tells you how a company finances its operations — primarily through borrowed money or through shareholders' funds.

**Formula:** D/E Ratio = Total Liabilities / Shareholders' Equity

## Calculating the D/E Ratio — An NGX Example

### Dangote Cement
- Total liabilities: ₦1.2 trillion
- Shareholders' equity: ₦1.8 trillion
- **D/E Ratio: 1.2 / 1.8 = 0.67**

This means for every ₦1 of shareholders' equity, Dangote Cement has ₦0.67 in debt. That is a moderate level — the company is not overly reliant on borrowed money.

### A Hypothetical High-Debt Company
- Total liabilities: ₦500 billion
- Shareholders' equity: ₦100 billion
- **D/E Ratio: 500 / 100 = 5.0**

This company has ₦5 in debt for every ₦1 of equity. That is very high leverage and carries significant risk.

## What the Numbers Mean

| D/E Ratio | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Below 0.5 | Conservative — the company uses little debt |
| 0.5 – 1.0 | Moderate — a balanced mix of debt and equity |
| 1.0 – 2.0 | Above average — the company leans on debt for growth |
| Above 2.0 | High leverage — risky unless the industry typically carries high debt |
| Above 5.0 | Very high — potential financial distress |

**Important:** What counts as "normal" varies by industry. Banks naturally have high D/E ratios (often 5-10x) because they take deposits (which are liabilities) as their core business model. A D/E of 8.0 for **Zenith Bank** is normal. A D/E of 8.0 for a cement company would be alarming.

## Why the D/E Ratio Matters

### 1. Risk Assessment
Higher debt means higher fixed obligations (interest payments). If a company's revenue drops — due to economic downturn, Naira devaluation, or industry challenges — a heavily indebted company might struggle to pay its debts. A low-debt company has more room to survive tough times.

### 2. Dividend Safety
Companies with excessive debt often have to prioritise loan repayments over dividend payments. If you are investing for dividends on the NGX, check the D/E ratio — a company drowning in debt might cut its dividend.

### 3. Growth Capacity
Some debt is good — it can fuel growth. If Dangote Cement borrows at 15% interest but earns 25% on the projects it finances, the debt is creating value. The key is whether the company earns more from borrowed money than it costs to borrow.

## What This Means for You

Before buying any stock on the NGX, check the debt-to-equity ratio and compare it to industry peers. A company with a much higher D/E ratio than its competitors might be taking on too much risk. Focus on companies that use debt wisely — enough to grow but not so much that a downturn could sink them. You can find the D/E ratio in annual reports or through research from **Meristem**, **Stanbic IBTC**, **ARM Securities**, or **CardinalStone**.
