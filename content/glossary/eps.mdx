**Earnings Per Share (EPS)** is a financial metric that shows how much profit a company generates for each outstanding share of its stock. It is one of the most important indicators of a company's profitability and is widely used by investors on the Nigerian Exchange (NGX).

## The Formula

> **EPS = Net Profit / Total Shares Outstanding**

## A Nigerian Example

If **Zenith Bank** reports a net profit of ₦280 billion for the financial year and has 31.4 billion shares outstanding, the EPS would be:

**₦280,000,000,000 / 31,400,000,000 = ₦8.92**

This means that for every share of Zenith Bank you own, the company earned approximately ₦8.92 in profit during that period.

## Why EPS Matters

EPS is a key building block for other important metrics. The **P/E ratio**, for instance, is calculated by dividing the share price by the EPS. Without knowing the EPS, you cannot properly value a stock.

A growing EPS over multiple years is a strong signal. It means the company is becoming more profitable on a per-share basis. When analysing Nigerian stocks, investors look at EPS trends over three to five years to assess whether a company is consistently improving its performance.

## Types of EPS

- **Basic EPS**: Uses the total number of shares currently outstanding. This is the standard calculation.
- **Diluted EPS**: Accounts for shares that could be created through stock options, convertible bonds, or other instruments. Diluted EPS is always equal to or lower than basic EPS.

## Practical Tip

When reviewing a company's financial results on the NGX, the EPS figure is typically reported in the income statement or earnings release. Companies like **GTCO**, **Dangote Cement**, and **Stanbic IBTC** publish their EPS every quarter and at year-end. Tracking these numbers helps you identify companies that are growing their earnings and are likely to reward shareholders.
