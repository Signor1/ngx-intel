Imagine you walk into a shop at Computer Village in Ikeja and say, "I want that phone — I will pay whatever it costs right now." You are not negotiating or waiting for a discount. You just want the item immediately at today's price. That is exactly what a **market order** is on the stock market.

A **market order** is an instruction to buy or sell a stock immediately at the best available current price. When you place a market order on the NGX, you are telling your broker: "I want this stock now, at whatever price the market is offering."

## How Market Orders Work on the NGX

Let us say you want to buy shares of **Zenith Bank**, and the stock is currently trading at ₦35.50 per share. If you place a market order for 1,000 shares, your broker will buy those shares at or very close to ₦35.50. The trade happens almost instantly during market hours.

**NGX trading hours:** Monday to Friday, 10:00 AM to 2:30 PM (WAT)

The key advantage of a market order is **speed**. You are guaranteed to get the stock (as long as there are sellers), but you are not guaranteed the exact price. The price might shift slightly between the time you place the order and when it executes — especially for stocks that do not trade very frequently.

## When to Use a Market Order

Market orders make sense when:

- **You want to buy or sell immediately** — you have done your research and you are ready to act
- **The stock is highly liquid** — blue chips like Dangote Cement, MTN Nigeria, or GTCO have enough buyers and sellers that the price will not jump around much
- **Small price differences do not matter to you** — if you are investing for the long term, a ₦0.50 difference per share will not matter much on a stock you plan to hold for years

## The Risk of Market Orders

For stocks with **low trading volume** (not many people buying or selling), a market order can be tricky. If only a few shares are available at the current price, your order might be filled at a higher price than you expected. This is called **slippage**.

For example, if you place a market order for 5,000 shares of a small company, but only 1,000 shares are available at ₦10, the remaining 4,000 might be filled at ₦10.20, ₦10.50, or even higher.

## Market Order vs. Limit Order

| Feature | Market Order | Limit Order |
|---|---|---|
| Speed | Immediate | Only when price is reached |
| Price certainty | No guarantee on exact price | You set the price |
| Best for | Liquid blue chip stocks | Price-sensitive trades |

## What This Means for You

If you are a beginner using **Bamboo**, **Trove**, or **Chaka**, the default buy button usually places something close to a market order. This is perfectly fine for popular stocks. Just be aware that for less-traded stocks, you might want to use a **limit order** instead to control the price you pay.
