The **Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio** is one of the most widely used valuation metrics in stock analysis. It tells you how much investors are willing to pay for every Naira of a company's earnings. In simple terms, it measures whether a stock is expensive or cheap relative to its profits.

## The Formula

> **P/E Ratio = Current Share Price / Earnings Per Share (EPS)**

## A Nigerian Example

Suppose **GTCO** is trading at ₦40 per share and its earnings per share (EPS) is ₦5.00. The P/E ratio would be:

**₦40 / ₦5.00 = 8x**

This means investors are paying ₦8 for every ₦1 of profit the company generates. A P/E of 8x is generally considered reasonable on the NGX, especially for banking stocks.

## Interpreting the P/E Ratio

- **Low P/E (e.g., below 10x)**: The stock may be undervalued or the market expects slower future growth. Many Nigerian bank stocks trade at low P/E ratios.
- **High P/E (e.g., above 20x)**: The market expects strong future earnings growth. Companies like **MTN Nigeria** or **Nestle Nigeria** sometimes trade at higher P/E ratios because investors expect their earnings to keep growing.

However, a low P/E does not automatically mean a stock is a good buy. It could indicate underlying problems. Similarly, a high P/E does not always mean a stock is overpriced; it could reflect justified optimism about future performance.

## Comparing P/E Ratios

The P/E ratio is most useful when comparing companies within the same sector. For example, comparing Zenith Bank's P/E to GTCO's P/E gives meaningful insight. Comparing a bank's P/E to a cement company's P/E is less useful because different industries have different earnings profiles.

## Key Takeaway

The P/E ratio is a quick valuation check. Use it alongside other metrics like dividend yield and EPS for a more complete analysis.
