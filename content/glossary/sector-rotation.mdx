Think of Lagos traffic patterns. In the morning, everyone rushes to Victoria Island and Ikoyi. In the evening, the flow reverses towards the mainland. The roads do not change — the people just shift from one area to another based on the time of day. **Sector rotation** is the stock market equivalent: investors move their money from one industry sector to another based on where they expect the best returns.

**Sector rotation** is an investment strategy where money flows from one sector of the economy to another as economic conditions, interest rates, or market cycles change. It is not that investors stop investing — they simply redirect their money to sectors they believe will perform better in the current environment.

## NGX Sectors — The Main Playing Fields

The NGX is divided into several key sectors:

- **Banking** — GTCO, Zenith Bank, Access Holdings, UBA, Stanbic IBTC
- **Consumer Goods** — Nestlé Nigeria, BUA Foods, Dangote Sugar, Unilever
- **Industrial Goods** — Dangote Cement, BUA Cement, Lafarge Africa
- **Oil & Gas** — Seplat Energy, Total Energies Nigeria, Oando
- **Insurance** — AXA Mansard, Custodian Investment, AIICO Insurance
- **Telecoms** — MTN Nigeria, Airtel Africa

At any point in time, some sectors are in favour while others are out of favour. This constant shifting is sector rotation in action.

## How Sector Rotation Works on the NGX

### Economic Growth Phase
When Nigeria's economy is expanding — GDP growing, consumer spending up — sectors like **banking** and **consumer goods** tend to do well. Banks lend more and earn more interest. People buy more household products, boosting companies like Nestlé and BUA Foods.

### Rising Interest Rates
When the CBN raises interest rates, **banking stocks** often benefit because they earn more on loans. However, **industrial companies** with heavy debt might struggle because borrowing costs increase.

### Rising Oil Prices
When crude oil prices surge globally, **oil and gas stocks** like Seplat Energy attract attention. Revenue and profits for oil producers jump. At the same time, the extra dollar inflows into Nigeria can strengthen the economy, eventually benefiting other sectors too.

### Defensive Periods
During uncertain times — elections, policy changes, global crises — investors often rotate into "defensive" sectors like **telecoms** (people still need phone service) and **consumer staples** (people still need food and soap).

## A Real NGX Example

In recent years, as the Naira depreciated significantly, investors rotated heavily into **industrial goods** (cement companies) because Dangote Cement and BUA Cement earn revenue in Naira but their products are essential regardless of currency movements. Meanwhile, **consumer goods** companies that import raw materials faced margin pressure, and some investors rotated out of that sector.

## How to Spot Sector Rotation

Watch for these signs:
- **Volume shifts** — suddenly, trading volume in banking stocks drops while oil stocks see unusual activity
- **Relative performance** — one sector keeps outperforming others week after week
- **News catalysts** — CBN policy changes, budget announcements, oil price movements, or new regulations

## What This Means for You

You do not need to constantly jump between sectors — that is more for active traders. But understanding sector rotation helps you make better long-term decisions. If you know that banking stocks tend to do well when interest rates rise, you can position yourself accordingly. Follow sector performance data from the **NGX website**, broker reports from **ARM Securities** or **Chapel Hill Denham**, and market commentary on platforms like **Bamboo** and **Trove** to stay informed about where money is flowing.
