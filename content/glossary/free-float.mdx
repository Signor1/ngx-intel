Imagine a landlord owns a building with 100 flats. He lives in 10, his family occupies 30, and 60 are rented out to the public. Those 60 publicly available flats are the "free float" — the portion available for regular people to rent. The same concept applies to shares on the stock market.

**Free float** (also called public float) is the number of a company's shares that are available for trading by the general public on the stock exchange. It excludes shares held by company insiders, founders, government, and other strategic holders who are unlikely to sell their shares on the open market.

**Formula:** Free Float = Total Shares Outstanding - Restricted/Insider-Held Shares

## Why Free Float Matters

### 1. It Affects Liquidity
A company with a large free float has more shares available for buying and selling, which means tighter bid-ask spreads and easier trading. A low free float means fewer shares circulating, which can make the stock harder to trade and more volatile.

### 2. It Impacts the ASI
The NGX uses **free-float adjusted market capitalisation** to determine a company's weight in certain indices. A company might have a massive total market cap, but if most shares are locked up with insiders, its influence on the index is reduced.

### 3. It Signals Control
A low free float often means the founders or controlling shareholders have a tight grip on the company. This can be good (strong leadership) or bad (minority shareholders have little influence).

## Free Float on the NGX — Real Examples

| Company | Approximate Free Float |
|---|---|
| GTCO | ~80% (widely held by the public) |
| Dangote Cement | ~15% (Dangote Group holds ~85%) |
| MTN Nigeria | ~22% (MTN Group SA holds majority) |
| BUA Cement | ~10% (BUA Group holds ~90%) |
| Zenith Bank | ~75% (widely distributed) |

Notice the stark differences. **GTCO** and **Zenith Bank** have high free floats, meaning their shares are widely available and trade with good liquidity. **Dangote Cement** and **BUA Cement** have very low free floats because the founding families hold dominant stakes. This is why Dangote Cement, despite being one of Africa's largest companies, sometimes has relatively low daily trading volume.

## The Free Float Problem on the NGX

One of the structural challenges of the NGX is that many listed companies have low free floats. When founders and strategic investors hold 70-90% of shares, there is not much left for the public to trade. This can lead to:

- **Low liquidity** — not enough shares changing hands daily
- **Price volatility** — a single large buy or sell order can move the stock price significantly
- **Wide bid-ask spreads** — the gap between buying and selling prices gets wider

The NGX has been working to address this by encouraging companies to increase their free float through regulatory requirements.

## What This Means for You

Before investing in any NGX stock, check the free float. Stocks with higher free floats (above 30-40%) are generally easier to buy and sell without dramatically affecting the price. If you invest in a low free float stock, be prepared for potentially volatile price swings and the possibility that it might take longer to sell your shares. You can find free float information on the **NGX website**, in company annual reports, and through research from brokers like **Meristem**, **ARM Securities**, and **Stanbic IBTC**.
