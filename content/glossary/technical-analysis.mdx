Imagine you sell mangoes at Mile 12 Market. Over the years, you have noticed patterns — prices always spike in January, dip in June, and rise again in September. You do not need to know why (weather, supply chains, demand shifts). You just know the pattern, and you use it to decide when to buy and sell. That is the spirit of **technical analysis** — studying patterns in stock prices and trading activity to predict where prices might go next.

**Technical analysis** is a method of evaluating stocks by analyzing historical price data, charts, and trading volume. Unlike fundamental analysis (which looks at a company's financials), technical analysis focuses purely on **price action** — the movement of a stock's price over time and the patterns that emerge.

The core belief: all relevant information about a stock — earnings, news, sentiment — is already reflected in its price. So by studying the price, you are studying everything.

## The Three Pillars of Technical Analysis

### 1. The Market Discounts Everything
Every piece of information — from annual reports to rumours — is priced into the stock. If Dangote Cement announces record profits, the stock price already reflects this before most people can act.

### 2. Prices Move in Trends
Stocks do not move randomly. They tend to follow trends — upward (bullish), downward (bearish), or sideways. Technical analysis helps you identify these trends early.

### 3. History Repeats Itself
Human psychology drives markets. Fear and greed create predictable patterns that repeat across time and across stocks.

## Key Tools of Technical Analysis

### Price Charts
The foundation of all technical analysis. There are several types:
- **Line charts** — simple plot of closing prices over time
- **Candlestick charts** — show the open, high, low, and close for each trading period. Widely used on the NGX
- **Bar charts** — similar to candlesticks but with a different visual format

### Support and Resistance
Key price levels where a stock tends to stop falling (support) or stop rising (resistance). More on this in the **support-resistance** glossary entry.

### Moving Averages
Smoothed lines that show the average price over a period (e.g., 50-day or 200-day moving average). They help you see the underlying trend through daily noise.

### Volume Analysis
High volume confirms price moves. A stock breaking above resistance on heavy volume is more significant than one doing so on thin trading.

### Chart Patterns
Recognisable shapes that appear on price charts and suggest future moves:
- **Head and Shoulders** — often signals a reversal from bullish to bearish
- **Double Bottom** — suggests a stock has found a floor and might rise
- **Triangle** — suggests a breakout is coming, but direction is uncertain

## Technical Analysis on the NGX — Does It Work?

Technical analysis works best on stocks with high liquidity and regular trading activity. On the NGX, this means it is most applicable to blue chips like **GTCO**, **Zenith Bank**, **Dangote Cement**, **MTN Nigeria**, and **BUA Cement** — stocks that trade millions of shares daily.

For smaller, less liquid stocks on the NGX, technical analysis is less reliable because a single large order can distort the price and create false patterns.

## An NGX Example

Suppose you are watching **MTN Nigeria**. The stock has bounced off ₦220 three times in the past six months (support level) and has struggled to break above ₦280 (resistance level). A technical analyst would say: "Buy near ₦220 with a stop-loss below ₦210, and consider selling near ₦275." If the stock finally breaks above ₦280 on high volume, it could be the start of a new upward trend.

## Technical vs. Fundamental Analysis

| Aspect | Technical Analysis | Fundamental Analysis |
|---|---|---|
| Focuses on | Price charts and patterns | Company financials |
| Time horizon | Short to medium term | Medium to long term |
| Key tools | Charts, indicators, volume | Annual reports, ratios, earnings |
| Best for | Timing entry and exit points | Selecting which stocks to buy |

Many successful investors use both: fundamental analysis to pick quality stocks, and technical analysis to time when to buy and sell them.

## What This Means for You

Technical analysis is a powerful skill, but it takes practice. As a beginner, start by learning to read basic price charts, identify support and resistance levels, and understand moving averages. Most broker platforms — including **Bamboo**, **Trove**, and research tools from **Meristem** and **Stanbic IBTC** — provide charts you can study. Do not rely on technical analysis alone for the NGX — always combine it with an understanding of the company's fundamentals.
