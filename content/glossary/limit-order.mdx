Imagine you are at Balogun Market looking for a particular fabric. The seller says ₦5,000 per yard, but you know it sometimes drops to ₦4,000 during slow periods. So you tell a trusted friend who works nearby: "If the price drops to ₦4,000, buy three yards for me." You have just placed a **limit order** — an instruction to buy only at a specific price or better.

A **limit order** is an instruction to buy or sell a stock at a specific price you choose, or better. Unlike a market order (which executes immediately at the current price), a limit order waits patiently until the market reaches your target price.

## How Limit Orders Work on the NGX

Let us say **MTN Nigeria** is currently trading at ₦260 per share, but you believe ₦245 is a fairer price. You place a **buy limit order** at ₦245 for 200 shares. Your order sits in the system and waits. If MTNN's price drops to ₦245 or lower during trading hours, your order executes automatically. If the price never drops that low, your order stays unfilled.

The same works in reverse. If you own shares of **Zenith Bank** at ₦35 and want to sell at ₦40, you place a **sell limit order** at ₦40. Your shares will only be sold when the price reaches ₦40 or higher.

## When to Use a Limit Order

Limit orders are your best friend when:

- **You have a target price in mind** — you have done your research and know what a stock is worth to you
- **You are not in a rush** — you are willing to wait days or weeks for the right price
- **The stock is volatile or thinly traded** — limit orders protect you from paying too much or selling too cheaply
- **You want to avoid slippage** — on less liquid NGX stocks, a limit order ensures you get the price you want

## The Downside

The risk of a limit order is simple: **your order might never execute**. If you set a buy limit at ₦245 and the stock never drops below ₦255, you miss out entirely. The market does not care about your preferred price.

This is the trade-off: market orders guarantee execution but not price; limit orders guarantee price but not execution.

## A Practical Example

Chidi wants to invest ₦100,000 in **BUA Cement**, currently trading at ₦105 per share. He could buy about 952 shares immediately with a market order. But Chidi has been watching the stock and noticed it often dips to around ₦98 during sell-offs. He places a limit order at ₦98, which would get him 1,020 shares — 68 more shares for the same money. Two weeks later, the market dips and his order fills at ₦98. Patience paid off.

## How to Place Limit Orders

Most trading platforms on the NGX support limit orders:

- On **Bamboo** and **Trove**, look for the option to set a "price" or "limit" when placing an order
- On **traditional broker platforms** like Meristem or Stanbic IBTC, you can specify your limit price when submitting a trade instruction
- Some brokers also accept limit orders via phone call or email

## What This Means for You

If you are an investor who likes to plan and is not in a hurry, limit orders are a powerful tool. They let you buy stocks at prices you are comfortable with and sell at prices that give you decent returns. As a rule of thumb: use market orders for blue chip stocks you want to own immediately, and limit orders when you are hunting for a bargain.
