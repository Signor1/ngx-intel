Imagine your friend wants to sell you his small shop in Surulere. The shop contains inventory, equipment, and furniture worth ₦3 million (that is the "book value" — what the assets are worth on paper). But your friend is asking ₦4.5 million because the shop has loyal customers and a great location. You are paying 1.5 times the value of what is physically there. That multiplier is essentially the **price-to-book ratio**.

The **Price-to-Book (P/B) ratio** compares a company's market price to its book value — the net value of its assets minus its liabilities as recorded on the balance sheet. It tells you how much the market is willing to pay for each Naira of a company's net assets.

**Formula:** P/B Ratio = Share Price / Book Value Per Share

Where: Book Value Per Share = (Total Assets - Total Liabilities) / Shares Outstanding

## How to Calculate It — An NGX Example

Let us say **GTCO** has the following:
- Share price: ₦42
- Total assets: ₦6 trillion
- Total liabilities: ₦5.1 trillion
- Shareholders' equity (book value): ₦900 billion
- Shares outstanding: 29.4 billion
- Book value per share: ₦900 billion / 29.4 billion = ₦30.61

**P/B Ratio:** ₦42 / ₦30.61 = **1.37x**

This means the market is valuing GTCO at 1.37 times the value of its net assets.

## What the P/B Ratio Tells You

| P/B Ratio | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Below 1.0 | Stock is trading below its net asset value — could be undervalued or a sign of trouble |
| 1.0 – 2.0 | Fairly valued — common for banks and industrial companies |
| 2.0 – 5.0 | Premium valuation — market expects strong future growth |
| Above 5.0 | Very expensive relative to assets — typical of high-growth tech companies |

### P/B Below 1.0 — A Bargain or a Trap?
A P/B ratio below 1.0 means you can theoretically buy the company for less than its assets are worth — like buying that shop for less than the value of the furniture inside it. On the NGX, some banks have traded below a P/B of 1.0, which attracted value investors. But be careful: sometimes a low P/B means the market believes the assets are not worth what the books say (perhaps bad loans in a bank's portfolio).

## Where P/B Ratio Works Best

The P/B ratio is especially useful for:

- **Banks** — Nigerian banks like Zenith Bank, GTCO, UBA, and Access Holdings are asset-heavy businesses where P/B is a primary valuation tool
- **Insurance companies** — Stanbic IBTC, AXA Mansard
- **Real estate and holding companies**

It is less useful for:
- **Technology and service companies** — whose value comes from intellectual property and human capital, not physical assets
- **Consumer brands** — where brand value is not reflected on the balance sheet

## P/B Ratios of Nigerian Banks

Nigerian banks have historically traded at P/B ratios between 0.5x and 2.0x. When a quality bank drops below 1.0x, experienced investors often see it as a buying opportunity — assuming the bank's assets are genuinely sound.

## What This Means for You

The P/B ratio is a powerful tool for evaluating bank stocks on the NGX. Before buying shares of any Nigerian bank, check its P/B ratio and compare it to industry peers. A bank trading at 0.8x P/B might be a better value than one trading at 2.0x — but always check the quality of its assets first. You can find book value data in annual reports or from research by **Meristem**, **ARM Securities**, or **CardinalStone**.
