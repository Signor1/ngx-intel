Walk into any market in Nigeria — say, Mile 12 or Ogunpa — and watch how buying and selling works. A tomato seller offers her basket at ₦3,000 (that is her **asking price**). A buyer counters with ₦2,500 (that is his **bid price**). The gap between those two prices — ₦500 — is the **bid-ask spread**. The same thing happens with stocks on the NGX, just with electronic orders instead of face-to-face haggling.

The **bid-ask spread** is the difference between the highest price a buyer is willing to pay (the **bid**) and the lowest price a seller is willing to accept (the **ask**). Every stock on the NGX has a bid price and an ask price at any given moment.

## Breaking It Down

- **Bid price** = the highest price someone is currently willing to pay to buy the stock
- **Ask price** (also called the offer price) = the lowest price someone is currently willing to accept to sell the stock
- **Spread** = Ask price minus Bid price

### A Nigerian Example

**Zenith Bank** trading data might look like this:

- Bid: ₦35.00 (buyers willing to pay ₦35.00)
- Ask: ₦35.30 (sellers want at least ₦35.30)
- Spread: ₦0.30

If you place a **market order to buy**, you pay the ask price (₦35.30). If you place a **market order to sell**, you receive the bid price (₦35.00). The ₦0.30 difference is the cost of immediate execution.

## Why the Spread Matters

### It Is a Hidden Cost
The spread is essentially a transaction cost that is not listed as a fee. If you buy at ₦35.30 and immediately want to sell, the best you can get is ₦35.00 — you lose ₦0.30 per share right away. On 1,000 shares, that is ₦300 gone before the stock even moves.

### It Tells You About Liquidity
- **Tight spread (small gap)** = highly liquid stock with many buyers and sellers. Blue chips like GTCO, Dangote Cement, and MTN Nigeria typically have tight spreads.
- **Wide spread (large gap)** = fewer buyers and sellers, less liquid stock. Many smaller companies on the NGX have wide spreads, sometimes ₦1 to ₦5 or more.

## Typical Spreads on the NGX

| Stock | Typical Spread | Liquidity |
|---|---|---|
| GTCO | ₦0.10 – ₦0.50 | Very high |
| Dangote Cement | ₦0.50 – ₦2.00 | High |
| MTN Nigeria | ₦0.50 – ₦2.00 | High |
| Small-cap stocks | ₦1.00 – ₦10.00+ | Low |

## How to Deal with the Spread

1. **Trade liquid stocks** — they have tighter spreads, which means lower hidden costs
2. **Use limit orders** — instead of accepting the ask price, set your own price between the bid and ask. You might get a better deal
3. **Be patient with illiquid stocks** — if the spread is wide, a limit order placed in the middle might attract a match
4. **Check the spread before trading** — if the spread is 5% or more of the stock price, you need a significant price move just to break even

## What This Means for You

The bid-ask spread is one of those quiet costs that many beginners overlook. When you trade on **Bamboo**, **Trove**, or **Chaka**, the price you see is usually the ask price (for buying) or bid price (for selling). Knowing the spread exists helps you make smarter decisions — especially when trading less popular stocks on the NGX.
