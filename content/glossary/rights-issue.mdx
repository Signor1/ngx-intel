Imagine you and three friends co-own a small business — a car wash. The business is growing and you need ₦2 million to add a second location. Instead of borrowing from a bank or bringing in a stranger, you offer each co-owner the chance to contribute more money in proportion to their current stake. If you own 25%, you have the right to put in 25% of the ₦2 million. That is a **rights issue**.

A **rights issue** is when a company that is already listed on the stock exchange offers its existing shareholders the opportunity to buy additional new shares, usually at a discounted price, before offering them to anyone else. It is a way for companies to raise fresh capital while giving current shareholders the first opportunity to maintain or increase their ownership.

## How a Rights Issue Works on the NGX

Here is a typical rights issue scenario:

1. **The company announces the rights issue** — for example, "1-for-4 rights issue at ₦15 per share" means you can buy 1 new share for every 4 shares you already own, at ₦15 per share
2. **Qualification date** — you must be a shareholder on a specific date to be eligible
3. **Offer period** — you typically have 2-4 weeks to decide whether to participate
4. **Payment** — you pay for the new shares through your broker or the receiving bank
5. **New shares are issued** — the new shares are credited to your CSCS account

### A Real Example: Access Bank

When **Access Bank** (now **Access Holdings**) needed to raise capital for its expansion plans across Africa, it conducted a rights issue. Existing shareholders were given the right to buy new shares at a price below the current market price. Shareholders who participated got discounted shares. Those who did not saw their ownership percentage diluted.

## The Discount — Why It Matters

The rights issue price is almost always set **below the current market price**. This discount is the incentive for shareholders to participate. If a stock is trading at ₦20 and the rights issue price is ₦15, you are effectively getting a 25% discount on new shares.

## Your Three Options When a Rights Issue Happens

If you own shares in a company that announces a rights issue, you have three choices:

### Option 1: Exercise Your Rights (Participate)
Buy the new shares at the discounted price. This is usually the best option if you believe in the company's future. You maintain your ownership percentage and get shares at a bargain.

### Option 2: Sell Your Rights
In some cases, you can sell your rights to someone else on the market. You do not buy the new shares, but you get paid for the right to buy them. This is called trading "nil paid rights."

### Option 3: Do Nothing (Let Them Lapse)
You can choose not to participate. But be aware — your ownership percentage in the company will decrease. This is called **dilution**. If the company had 1 billion shares and issues 250 million new ones, your percentage of the company shrinks if you do not buy new shares.

## Rights Issue vs. Bonus Shares

These are often confused but they are very different:

| Feature | Rights Issue | Bonus Shares |
|---|---|---|
| Cost | You pay for new shares (at a discount) | Completely free |
| Purpose | Raise money for the company | Reward existing shareholders |
| Effect on cash | You spend money | You spend nothing |

## What This Means for You

When a company you own shares in announces a rights issue, pay attention. If the company is solid and the funds are being raised for genuine growth, participating is usually a smart move — you get discounted shares in a business you already believe in. Check with your broker — **ARM Securities**, **Meristem**, **CardinalStone**, or your app like **Bamboo** or **Trove** — for the deadline and payment instructions. Missing the deadline means missing out on the discount.
