Imagine you are at a football match and you want to know which team the crowd supports. You could count how many fans are wearing green jerseys versus red jerseys. If green outnumbers red 3 to 1, the green team has dominant support. The **Advance/Decline (A/D) ratio** does the same for the stock market — it counts how many stocks went up versus how many went down to gauge overall market sentiment.

The **Advance/Decline ratio** is calculated by dividing the number of stocks that increased in price (advancers) by the number of stocks that decreased in price (decliners) during a trading session. It is one of the simplest and most popular measures of **market breadth**.

**Formula:** A/D Ratio = Number of Advancing Stocks / Number of Declining Stocks

## How to Read the A/D Ratio

| A/D Ratio | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Above 2.0 | Strong bullish breadth — the majority of stocks are rising |
| 1.0 – 2.0 | Moderately positive — more stocks up than down |
| Exactly 1.0 | Neutral — equal number of gainers and losers |
| 0.5 – 1.0 | Moderately negative — more stocks falling than rising |
| Below 0.5 | Strong bearish breadth — widespread selling |

## An NGX Example

Let us say the NGX daily report shows:
- **Advancers:** 40 stocks
- **Decliners:** 12 stocks
- **Unchanged:** 90 stocks

**A/D Ratio:** 40 / 12 = **3.33**

This is a strongly bullish reading. It tells you that for every stock that fell, more than three stocks rose. The market rally is broad-based and genuine, not just driven by one or two big stocks.

Now consider another day:
- **Advancers:** 8 stocks
- **Decliners:** 45 stocks

**A/D Ratio:** 8 / 45 = **0.18**

This is deeply bearish. Sellers are dominating across the board.

## How Investors Use the A/D Ratio

### Confirming Market Trends
If the ASI is hitting new highs and the A/D ratio is consistently above 1.0, the uptrend is healthy and well-supported. If the ASI is rising but the A/D ratio is declining (fewer stocks participating in the rally), it could be a warning that the rally is losing steam.

### Spotting Divergences
One of the most powerful uses of the A/D ratio is spotting **divergences** — when the index and breadth move in opposite directions:

- **Bearish divergence:** ASI makes a new high, but the A/D ratio is declining. This suggests the rally is narrowing and might reverse.
- **Bullish divergence:** ASI makes a new low, but the A/D ratio is improving. This suggests selling pressure is easing and a recovery might be near.

### Timing Entry Points
Some investors use extreme A/D readings to time their entries. A very low A/D ratio (below 0.3) might signal overselling — a potential buying opportunity for brave investors.

## Where to Find A/D Data

The NGX publishes daily trading summaries showing the number of advancers and decliners. You can find this on:
- The **NGX website** (daily market reports)
- Research summaries from **Meristem**, **CardinalStone**, **Stanbic IBTC**, and **ARM Securities**
- Financial news platforms covering the Nigerian market

## What This Means for You

The A/D ratio takes two seconds to check and can save you from misreading the market. Before you celebrate a rising ASI or panic over a falling one, look at the advance/decline numbers. They tell you whether the entire market is moving — or just a few stocks creating an illusion. It is a simple tool that gives you an edge over investors who only watch the headline index.
