Imagine you want to know your personal net worth. You add up everything you own — your car (₦8 million), your savings (₦2 million), your electronics (₦500,000) — that is ₦10.5 million in assets. Then you subtract what you owe — a car loan (₦3 million), personal debts (₦500,000) — that is ₦3.5 million in liabilities. Your net worth is ₦7 million. For a company, this same calculation is called **Net Asset Value (NAV)**.

**Net Asset Value** is the total value of a company's assets minus its total liabilities. It represents the theoretical value shareholders would receive if the company sold everything it owned and paid off all its debts. It is essentially the company's "book value" or "net worth."

**Formula:** NAV = Total Assets - Total Liabilities

**NAV Per Share** = NAV / Total Shares Outstanding

## Calculating NAV — An NGX Example

Let us look at a simplified example using **Zenith Bank**:

- Total assets: ₦12 trillion
- Total liabilities: ₦10.5 trillion
- **NAV: ₦1.5 trillion**
- Shares outstanding: 31.4 billion
- **NAV per share: ₦47.77**

If Zenith Bank's stock price is ₦35, it means the market is valuing the company at less than its net asset value. This could be a value opportunity — or it could mean the market has concerns about the quality of those assets.

## Why NAV Matters

### 1. Valuation Benchmark
NAV gives you a baseline to compare against the stock price. If a stock trades significantly below its NAV per share, it might be undervalued. If it trades far above, the market is pricing in future growth or intangible value (brand, intellectual property, etc.).

### 2. Especially Important for Banks
On the NGX, NAV is one of the most critical metrics for evaluating banks. Nigerian banks hold massive amounts of assets (loans, investments, cash), and NAV tells you what shareholders are truly left with after all obligations are met.

### 3. Mutual Funds and ETFs
If you invest in Nigerian mutual funds (like ARM Aggressive Growth Fund or Stanbic IBTC Equity Fund), the NAV is calculated daily to determine the price of each unit you buy or sell.

## NAV for Different Types of Companies

| Company Type | How NAV is Used |
|---|---|
| Banks | Primary valuation tool alongside P/B ratio |
| Insurance companies | Key measure of financial strength |
| Real estate companies | Reflects the value of property holdings |
| Manufacturing | Useful but less critical than earnings-based metrics |
| Service companies | Less relevant — most value is in people and brands, not physical assets |

## Limitations of NAV

NAV is not perfect. It has some blind spots:

- **Assets may be overvalued on the books** — a bank might list loans as assets, but if borrowers default, those loans are worthless
- **It ignores future earnings potential** — a company's real value often comes from its ability to generate future profits, not just its current assets
- **Intangible assets are not fully captured** — a brand like MTN is worth billions, but that brand value is not fully reflected on the balance sheet

## What This Means for You

NAV is your "reality check" when evaluating a company. It tells you what the company is worth today based on what it owns and owes. For banking stocks on the NGX — which make up a large portion of what retail investors buy — knowing the NAV helps you avoid overpaying. Check NAV data in annual reports, on the NGX website, or through research from **CardinalStone**, **Chapel Hill Denham**, or **ARM Securities**.
