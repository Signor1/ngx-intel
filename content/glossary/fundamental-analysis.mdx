Imagine you want to buy a danfo bus as a business investment. You would not just look at the paint job and tyre shine. You would check the engine, inspect the gearbox, review the fuel consumption, look at the route it will run, and calculate how much money it can earn per day versus the maintenance costs. **Fundamental analysis** is doing this same thorough inspection — but for a company before you buy its stock.

**Fundamental analysis** is a method of evaluating a company by examining its financial statements, management quality, competitive position, and economic environment to determine whether its stock is fairly priced, undervalued, or overvalued. It answers the question: "Is this company actually worth the price the market is asking?"

## The Two Levels of Fundamental Analysis

### Quantitative Analysis (The Numbers)
This is the measurable, data-driven side. You look at financial statements and calculate ratios.

**Key metrics to check:**

| Metric | What It Tells You | Good Range for NGX |
|---|---|---|
| **P/E Ratio** | How expensive the stock is relative to earnings | 3 – 12 for banks; varies by sector |
| **EPS (Earnings Per Share)** | How much profit each share generates | Growing year over year |
| **ROE (Return on Equity)** | How efficiently the company uses shareholders' money | Above 15% |
| **Debt-to-Equity** | How leveraged the company is | Below 1.0 for non-banks |
| **Payout Ratio** | How much of profits go to dividends | 30% – 60% |
| **Revenue Growth** | Whether the company is growing sales | Positive and consistent |
| **Net Profit Margin** | How much of revenue becomes actual profit | Varies by industry |

### Qualitative Analysis (The Story)
Numbers alone do not tell the full story. You also evaluate:

- **Management quality** — who runs the company? What is their track record? Tony Elumelu's leadership at UBA, or Herbert Wigwe's legacy at Access Holdings, influenced investor confidence.
- **Competitive advantage** — does the company have something competitors cannot easily replicate? Dangote Cement's distribution network across Africa is a moat. MTN's massive subscriber base is another.
- **Industry dynamics** — is the industry growing or shrinking? Nigerian banking is being transformed by fintech. Cement demand grows with infrastructure development.
- **Regulatory environment** — CBN policies affect banks. NCC regulations affect telecoms. Government policies on fuel subsidies affect downstream oil companies.

## How to Analyze an NGX Company Step by Step

Let us walk through analyzing **Zenith Bank** as an example:

### Step 1: Read the Annual Report
Download Zenith Bank's latest annual report from their website. Focus on revenue, net profit, and EPS trends over the past 3-5 years.

### Step 2: Check Key Ratios
- **P/E Ratio:** If Zenith trades at ₦35 with EPS of ₦8, the P/E is 4.4x — low compared to global banks but normal for the NGX
- **ROE:** If Zenith reports 22% ROE, that is excellent — the bank is efficient
- **Payout Ratio:** If Zenith pays out 40% of earnings as dividends, that is healthy and sustainable

### Step 3: Evaluate Management and Strategy
Is the bank expanding its digital channels? Growing its retail deposits? Reducing non-performing loans? These qualitative factors determine whether the numbers will keep improving.

### Step 4: Compare with Peers
How does Zenith stack up against GTCO, UBA, and Access Holdings on the same metrics? Comparative analysis reveals which bank offers the best value.

### Step 5: Determine Fair Value
Using all the above, estimate what the stock should be worth. If your analysis suggests Zenith is worth ₦45 but it trades at ₦35, it may be undervalued — a potential buying opportunity.

## Where to Get Data for Fundamental Analysis

- **Annual reports** — the primary source, available on company websites
- **NGX website** — financial disclosures from all listed companies
- **Broker research** — **ARM Securities**, **Meristem**, **CardinalStone**, **Stanbic IBTC**, and **Chapel Hill Denham** publish detailed company reports
- **Investment apps** — **Bamboo**, **Trove**, and **Chaka** provide basic financial data within their platforms

## Fundamental vs. Technical Analysis

Fundamental analysis tells you **what to buy** — which companies are strong, fairly priced, and worth owning. Technical analysis tells you **when to buy** — the optimal entry and exit points based on price patterns. The best investors on the NGX use both.

## What This Means for You

Fundamental analysis is the most important skill you can develop as a stock investor. It turns you from a gambler ("I heard this stock will go up") into an informed investor ("I have reviewed the numbers and believe this company is undervalued"). Start simple — check the P/E, EPS, and dividend trend for any stock before buying. As you get more comfortable, add ROE, debt-to-equity, and qualitative analysis to your toolkit. The time you spend understanding a company before investing will pay off many times over.
