Imagine you own a successful suya spot that has grown from one stand to twenty locations across Lagos. Business is booming, but you need ₦500 million to expand nationwide. Instead of going to the bank for a loan, you decide to invite the public to become co-owners by buying shares in your business. That is essentially what an **IPO** is.

**IPO** stands for **Initial Public Offering**. It is the very first time a private company sells its shares to the general public on a stock exchange. Before an IPO, a company is "private" — owned by founders, early investors, and perhaps some employees. After an IPO, anyone with a brokerage account can buy and own a piece of that company.

## How an IPO Works

The IPO process on the NGX typically follows these steps:

1. **The company decides to go public** — usually to raise money for expansion, pay off debt, or give early investors a way to cash out
2. **They hire an investment bank** — firms like **Stanbic IBTC**, **Chapel Hill Denham**, or **CardinalStone** help prepare the company and set the initial share price
3. **SEC approval** — the Securities and Exchange Commission reviews all the documents to protect investors
4. **The offer period** — the company opens the offer to the public for a set period (usually 2-4 weeks). You can apply to buy shares through your stockbroker or investment app
5. **Listing day** — the shares begin trading on the NGX. From this day, you can buy and sell the shares freely

## Famous Nigerian IPOs

### MTN Nigeria (2019)
MTN Nigeria's listing was a landmark event. While technically a "listing by introduction" (not a traditional IPO with new shares sold), it made MTN available to everyday Nigerian investors for the first time. The stock listed at ₦90 per share and has since grown significantly, rewarding early believers.

### BUA Foods (2022)
**BUA Foods** — the company behind BUA Sugar and BUA Flour — listed on the NGX and quickly became one of the most valuable companies on the exchange. Investors who got in early saw impressive returns.

### Airtel Africa (2019)
Airtel Africa listed on both the London Stock Exchange and the NGX, giving Nigerian investors access to one of Africa's largest telecoms companies.

## Should You Invest in an IPO?

IPOs can be exciting, but they are not guaranteed wins. Here is the honest picture:

**Potential advantages:**
- You get in at the "ground floor" price
- Strong companies can see significant price appreciation after listing
- IPOs generate market excitement and media attention

**Potential risks:**
- The company might be overvalued at the IPO price
- There is limited trading history to analyze
- Post-IPO prices can drop below the offer price

A good rule of thumb: only invest in IPOs of companies whose products or services you understand and whose financials look solid.

## How to Participate in an IPO

When an IPO is announced on the NGX, you can apply through:
- Your **stockbroker** (ARM Securities, Meristem, CardinalStone, etc.)
- **Digital platforms** like Bamboo, Trove, or Chaka (if they support the offer)
- **Receiving banks** designated by the company conducting the IPO

You fill out an application form, specify how many shares you want, and transfer the payment. If the offer is oversubscribed (more people want shares than are available), you may receive fewer shares than you applied for.

## What This Means for You

Keep an eye on upcoming IPOs on the NGX — they can be great opportunities to invest in strong companies early. But do your homework first. Read the prospectus, understand the business, and never invest more than you can afford to lock up for a while.
