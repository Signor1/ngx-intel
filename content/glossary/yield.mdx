Imagine you put ₦500,000 in a savings account and the bank pays you ₦25,000 in interest over a year. The rate of return — how much your money earned expressed as a percentage — is 5%. In the world of investing, this percentage return is called **yield**.

**Yield** is the income earned on an investment, expressed as a percentage of the investment's cost or current value. It answers a simple question: "For every Naira I put in, how much am I getting back in income?"

## Types of Yield

### Dividend Yield (Stocks)
This is the most common type of yield for stock investors. It measures how much a company pays in dividends relative to its share price.

**Formula:** Dividend Yield = (Annual Dividend Per Share / Share Price) x 100

If **Zenith Bank** pays a dividend of ₦3.50 per share and the stock price is ₦35, the dividend yield is (3.50 / 35) x 100 = **10%**. That means for every ₦100 you invest in Zenith Bank, you earn ₦10 per year in dividends alone — without selling your shares.

### Bond Yield (Fixed Income)
If you buy Nigerian government bonds or corporate bonds, yield measures the annual interest you receive relative to what you paid. For example, an FGN (Federal Government of Nigeria) bond might offer a yield of 14-18%, depending on market conditions.

### Earnings Yield (Inverse of P/E)
This is less common for beginners but useful: it measures a company's earnings relative to its share price. It is essentially the P/E ratio flipped upside down.

## High Yield vs. Low Yield — What to Watch For

A high yield is not always a good thing. Sometimes a stock's yield is high because the **share price has crashed**, not because the company is generous. This is called a **yield trap**. If a company's stock drops from ₦50 to ₦10, and they still pay ₦3 in dividends, the yield looks amazing (30%) — but the company might be in serious trouble and could cut the dividend entirely.

Always ask: **Is this yield sustainable?** Check the company's earnings and **payout ratio** to be sure.

## What This Means for You

Yield helps you compare different investments side by side. Is your money better off in a fixed deposit earning 12% or in a stock like GTCO yielding 9% in dividends but with additional potential for capital gains? That is the kind of decision yield helps you make. As a beginner, aim for stocks with moderate, sustainable yields from profitable companies rather than chasing the highest number. You can check yield data on **Bamboo**, **Trove**, or the NGX website.
