Imagine bouncing a ball on a concrete floor. No matter how many times you drop it, it hits the floor and bounces back up. The floor is the **support** — it prevents the ball from falling further. Now imagine throwing that ball at a concrete ceiling. It hits the ceiling and comes back down. The ceiling is the **resistance** — it prevents the ball from going higher. In the stock market, support and resistance are price levels that act as floors and ceilings.

**Support** is a price level where a stock tends to stop falling because buyers step in. **Resistance** is a price level where a stock tends to stop rising because sellers emerge. Together, they create a price range within which a stock bounces back and forth until something significant happens.

## How Support Forms

Support levels form because of collective investor behavior. When a stock drops to a certain price and buyers consistently step in, a floor is established. Here is why it happens:

- **Value buyers** think the stock is cheap at that price and start buying
- **Previous buyers** who missed the stock at that level before are now ready to buy
- **Psychological round numbers** — ₦10, ₦50, ₦100, ₦200 — naturally attract buying interest

### NGX Example: Zenith Bank
If Zenith Bank drops to ₦32 three times over six months and bounces back each time, ₦32 is a strong support level. Investors now expect that ₦32 is a "safe" price point, and buying pressure consistently appears there.

## How Resistance Forms

Resistance works in reverse. When a stock rises to a certain price and sellers consistently appear, a ceiling is established:

- **Profit-takers** who bought lower sell at this level to lock in gains
- **Trapped buyers** who bought at this price previously and saw it fall are eager to sell and "break even"
- **Round number psychology** — sellers cluster around clean numbers

### NGX Example: Dangote Cement
If Dangote Cement rallies to ₦350 twice but fails to break through, ₦350 is a resistance level. Each time the stock approaches that price, selling pressure increases and pushes it back down.

## When Support and Resistance Break

The most important thing in technical analysis is what happens when these levels **break**:

### Support Breaks (Bearish)
When a stock falls below a support level on high volume, it is a bearish signal. The former support often becomes new resistance. If Zenith Bank breaks below ₦32 convincingly, the next support might be at ₦28, and ₦32 now becomes resistance.

### Resistance Breaks (Bullish)
When a stock breaks above resistance on high volume, it is a bullish signal — called a **breakout**. The former resistance often becomes new support. If Dangote Cement finally breaks above ₦350 with heavy trading, ₦350 now becomes the new floor, and the stock could run to ₦400 or beyond.

## Identifying Support and Resistance on NGX Charts

Look for these clues:

1. **Multiple touches** — the more times a stock bounces off a level, the stronger it is
2. **Volume at the level** — high volume at support/resistance makes it more significant
3. **Time frame** — levels that hold over weeks and months are stronger than those formed over a few days
4. **Round numbers** — stocks on the NGX often find support/resistance at clean prices like ₦10, ₦25, ₦50, ₦100
5. **Moving averages** — the 50-day and 200-day moving averages often act as dynamic support/resistance

## How to Trade Using Support and Resistance

### Basic Strategy for Beginners
- **Buy near support** — when a quality stock drops to a well-established support level, it can be a good entry point
- **Sell near resistance** — if you are looking to take profits, resistance levels are logical points to consider selling
- **Use breakouts** — if a stock breaks above strong resistance on high volume, consider buying for the new trend
- **Set stop-losses below support** — if support breaks, the stock could fall further, so protect yourself
